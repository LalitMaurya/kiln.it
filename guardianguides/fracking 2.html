The issue
1. The issue at a glance
Shale gas is natural gas stored deep underground in fine-grained sedimentary rocks. It can be extracted using a process known as hydraulic fracturing – "fracking" – which involves pumping huge quantities of pressurised water, sand and chemicals into the rocks. Advocates argue that exploiting the world’s vast shale gas deposits will help keep energy affordable and cut consumption of dirtier coal. But sceptics claim that fracking is dangerous and polluting, and that tapping into extra natural gas supplies will boost rather than reduce planet-warming carbon emissions.
Current situation
2. Why is it being talked about now?
Shale gas production and controversy are both increasing fast. In the US, where the technology was developed, fracking has scaled up quickly to account for around a quarter of the country’s natural gas extraction but critics say it could poison New York’s drinking water supply. Meanwhile, the first fracking project in the UK claimed to find trillions of cubic meters of gas but was put on hold after being linked to minor earthquakes. Due to these kinds of concerns, France and Bulgaria have banned fracking and campaigners are calling for other countries and US states to follow suit. But Australia has recently given fracking the go-ahead to, and China is making plans to further exploit its huge reserves.
History
3. A brief history
Shale gas was formed hundreds of millions of years ago from decomposing plants trapped by clay and silt. A small amount of gas was extracted from shallow shale layers in the 19th century, but it wasn’t until the 1970s that fracking technology started to develop in earnest, supported by US government R&D funding. After a slow start, American shale gas production started soaring in around 2005, leading to growing interest in the energy source around the world. By 2011, fracking experiments were underway in UK, China, Poland and elsewhere, and estimates of global shale-gas reserves were rising fast, with the outgoing head of Shell claiming that there could be enough to power the world for 200 years.
Technology
4. How does fracking work?
A hole is drilled down into shale rocks more than a kilometre below the surface and then extended horizontally through the shale layer. The wellhole is lined and then massive quantities of water, sand and chemicals are pumped down into the horizontal well at high pressure. This opens up tiny fissures in the shale, which are held open by the sand, enabling the trapped gas to escape. It bubbles out of the rock, aided by the injected chemicals, and is piped off at surface level. See a large graphic of a fracking site.

Controversy
5. Why is it controversial?
Fracking has been linked to a wide range of problems, the highest profile of which has been chemical contamination of underground aquifers. This connection has been acknowledged by the US Environmental Protection Agency in the case of a community in Wyoming that has had to stop drinking local water, though a recent University of Texas report said contamination was more likely to be caused by surface spills and therefore could be avoided. Either way, details of the chemicals used in fracking are only just starting to be disclosed, and their health impacts are not well understood. Other fracking risks include ecologically damaging levels of water consumption; minor earthquakes; emissions of explosive and climate-warming methane; and the impact of clearing forests for fracking sites.
Climate change
6. Is shale gas good or bad for climate change?
Creating heat or electricity by burning natural gas produces less CO2 (and other pollutants) than using coal or oil. Hence the gas industry and some commentators argue that shale gas is good news for the climate, as it could limit coal consumption while renewables and nuclear are scaling up. But the limited evidence that exists suggests that shale gas may be no better than coal, due to the large emissions of methane from fracking sites. Furthermore, as a recent Tyndall Centre report concluded, without a global CO2 cap, additional gas resources won’t necessarily reduce coal use and may instead cause overall fossil fuel use and carbon emissions to increase.
Reserves
7. How much shale gas is there?
The US government assessed 32 countries and concluded they had of 169 trillion cubic metres of technically recoverable shale gas (around the same as the global economically recoverable reserves of conventional natural gas), with the largest reserves in China, the US, Argentina and Mexico (see map). But the figures keep changing. Estimated US reserves doubled between 2010 and 2011, and the company drilling in Britain – Cuadrilla Resources – claims its site near Blackpool in Lancashire contains more than 5 trillion cubic meters, ten times more than the American government estimate for the whole of the UK. Similarly, China’s own survey recently put its reserves at 25 trillion cubic meters, nearly double the figure given in the US survey. But sceptics claim these figures may be overblown.
Significance
8. How significant is shale gas?
Shale gas could be hugely significant in the some regions, including the US. By 2035 the country expects fracking to account for 46% of its gas production and according to BP shale gas, tar sands and other ‘unconventional’ fossil fuel sources will make North and South America largely self-sufficient in energy by 2030. By contrast, a Deutsche Bank report looking at the potential for shale in Europe concluded that, due partly to higher population density and stronger environmental regulation, “those waiting for a shale gas 'revolution' outside the US will likely be disappointed, in terms of both price and the speed at which high-volume production can be achieved.” Much will depend how the debate about environmental impacts and regulation plays out in coming years. Fossil fuel prices will be another factor, as higher prices will make fracking more economical.
Key players
9. A few key players
• The EPA The US Environmental Protection Agency which has acknowledged the link to pollution but so far done very little to regulate American fracking

• Barack Obama. Insiders says the US administration hopes to rely on switching from coal to gas as its nod to global carbon reductions

• Cuadrilla Resources. The first company to frack UK. It put its Lancashire operation on hold after causing earthquakes, but is fiercely resisting a moratorium of fracking

• Charles Hendry. UK environment minister who says shale gas is “worth exploring”

• Dieter Helm. Oxford energy academic who advocates using gas to cheaply cut emissions

• Fracked off. The campaign group leading the charge against fracking in the UK
